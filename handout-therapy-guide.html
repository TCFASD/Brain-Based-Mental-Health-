A Caregiver's Guide to Mental Health Therapies

What each therapy asks the brain to do, whether caregivers are involved, and how to know if it might be a good fit for your child.

Before You Read: Five Things to Know

Understanding therapy options can feel overwhelming. Here are five ideas that can help you make sense of what you're reading — and ask better questions when you meet with providers.

"Top-Down" vs. "Bottom-Up" Matters

"Top-down" therapies (like CBT) ask the thinking brain to manage emotions and behavior. "Bottom-up" therapies (like Theraplay or Somatic Experiencing) start with the body and nervous system. For children with brain-based differences, bottom-up approaches are often more accessible — you have to calm the survival brain before the thinking brain can learn.

Caregiver Involvement Is Not Optional

The therapies with the strongest evidence for children with brain-based differences are all high-caregiver-involvement. A therapist who sees your child alone for 50 minutes a week cannot change the other 167 hours. Look for approaches where you are trained and supported as a partner.

"Can't" vs. "Won't" Changes Everything

Many standard approaches assume the child CAN do the expected behavior and just needs the right motivation. For brain-based differences, the behavior often reflects a genuine skill deficit or neurological barrier. The best-fit therapies are built on this understanding.

One Therapy Is Rarely Enough

Your child might benefit from a combination — for example, a parent training approach for you, a play-based therapy for your child's individual work, and a problem-solving framework like CPS for daily life. That is normal and often the most effective path.

Match the Therapy to the Brain, Not the Diagnosis

A child with FASD, a child with developmental trauma, and a child with autism may all benefit from similar approaches — because their brains need similar things even though their diagnoses differ. When evaluating a therapy, the most important question is: can my child's brain do what this therapy is asking?

Talk-Based and Cognitive Approaches

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

Ages 7+ Brain-Based Fit: Limited Without Modification
What It Asks the Brain to Do

CBT asks the brain to notice its own thoughts, evaluate whether those thoughts are accurate, and replace unhelpful thoughts with more helpful ones. It teaches the connection between thoughts, feelings, and behavior — and uses the thinking brain (prefrontal cortex) to regulate emotions. This is a "top-down" approach: it starts with thinking and works down to feelings and behavior.

Caregiver Involvement

Varies. Traditional CBT is often individual — the child or teen meets alone with the therapist. Some programs include parent sessions, but caregiver involvement is not built into the standard model.

How to Know If It Might Be a Good Fit
  • Your child can identify and talk about their thoughts and feelings
  • Your child can think abstractly (understanding cause and effect, "if/then" reasoning)
  • Anxiety, depression, or distorted thinking is the primary concern
  • Your child can remember and apply skills learned in session to real life
Brain-Based Consideration CBT relies on self-monitoring, abstract reasoning, working memory, and the ability to generalize skills to new situations — these are often the exact areas where brain-based differences create challenges. Children may appear to "learn" CBT skills in session but be unable to apply them in real life. If your child isn't responding to CBT, the first question should be "Can their brain do what this therapy is asking?" — not "Are they not trying hard enough?"

Trauma-Focused CBT (TF-CBT)

Ages 3–18 Brain-Based Fit: Moderate With Adaptation
What It Asks the Brain to Do

TF-CBT is a structured treatment specifically for children who have experienced trauma. It guides the brain through gradual trauma processing using the PRACTICE components: Psychoeducation, Relaxation, Affective modulation, Cognitive coping, Trauma narrative, In-vivo mastery, Conjoint sessions, and Enhancing safety. The child's brain gradually approaches and processes traumatic material in a safe, structured way — rather than avoiding it. It combines cognitive skills with body-based techniques like relaxation.

Caregiver Involvement

Very high — built into the model. Sessions are typically 90 minutes: 45 minutes with the child, 45 minutes with the caregiver. Caregivers receive all components in parallel and participate in conjoint sessions where the child shares their trauma narrative. This is a major strength.

How to Know If It Might Be a Good Fit
  • Your child has experienced trauma and is showing symptoms (nightmares, avoidance, hypervigilance, emotional dysregulation)
  • You want to be actively involved in your child's trauma treatment
  • Your child can engage with some level of verbal/cognitive processing (even if simplified)
  • The therapist is trained in both TF-CBT and neurodevelopmental adaptations
Brain-Based Consideration TF-CBT has the same cognitive demands as CBT plus the added requirement of creating a trauma narrative. However, the strong caregiver involvement and structured approach are significant strengths. For children with brain-based differences who carry trauma, a clinician experienced with both TF-CBT and neurodevelopmental differences should assess whether the child can engage with the cognitive and narrative components — and adapt accordingly.

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)

Teens primarily Brain-Based Fit: Moderate — Skills Are Useful
What It Asks the Brain to Do

DBT teaches four skill sets: Mindfulness (being present-focused), Distress Tolerance (surviving a crisis without making it worse), Emotion Regulation (understanding and managing emotions), and Interpersonal Effectiveness (navigating relationships). The adolescent version adds "Walking the Middle Path" for families. Unlike CBT, some DBT skills work through the body — distress tolerance techniques like ice, paced breathing, and intense exercise directly calm the nervous system without requiring the child to think their way through it.

Caregiver Involvement

Included in the adolescent version (DBT-A). Caregivers attend the skills group alongside the teen and learn the same skills. This means the whole family shares a common language for managing emotions and crises.

How to Know If It Might Be a Good Fit
  • Your teen has intense emotional reactions, self-harm, or difficulty managing crises
  • Emotional dysregulation is the primary concern
  • You want concrete, practical skills (not just "talk about feelings")
  • You are willing to learn and practice the skills alongside your teen
Brain-Based Consideration Full DBT is complex and demanding: weekly individual therapy, weekly skills group, between-session coaching calls, and homework. This is a heavy load for any family. The body-based distress tolerance skills are often the most accessible and effective piece for brain-based differences. A modified, simplified approach using selected DBT skills may work better than the full protocol. Ask the therapist: "Can we focus on the skills that match my child's abilities?"
Play-Based and Body-Based Approaches

Play Therapy

Includes: Child-Centered Play Therapy (CCPT), Directive Play Therapy, Filial Therapy
Ages 2–12+ Brain-Based Fit: Strong
What It Asks the Brain to Do

Play therapy works through the experiential and emotional brain — not through words or insight. Play is the natural language of childhood, and children under 10 typically lack the cognitive development for abstract talk therapy.

  • Child-Centered (CCPT): The child leads; the therapist follows, reflects emotions, and provides unconditional positive regard. The brain expresses and processes emotions through play rather than words.
  • Directive: The therapist introduces specific activities (sand tray, art, puppets, structured games) to address identified issues. The brain engages with specific content through accessible, concrete means.
  • Filial Therapy: The therapist trains the caregiver to conduct child-centered play sessions. The caregiver learns to follow the child's lead, reflect emotions, and set limits — strengthening the attachment bond through play.
Caregiver Involvement

Varies dramatically by type. CCPT traditionally has low caregiver involvement (the therapy happens between child and therapist). Filial Therapy has very high caregiver involvement — the caregiver becomes the therapeutic agent. If caregiver involvement matters to you (and for brain-based differences, it should), ask specifically about Filial Therapy.

How to Know If It Might Be a Good Fit
  • Your child is young or has developmental delays that make talk therapy difficult
  • Your child communicates better through action and play than through words
  • Emotional expression, processing, or attachment is the primary concern
  • Previous talk-based therapies haven't connected with your child
Brain-Based Consideration Play therapy may be the most accessible individual therapy modality for children with brain-based differences because it works through the experiential brain, bypasses verbal and cognitive demands, and meets children at their developmental (not chronological) level. Filial Therapy is especially promising because it strengthens the caregiver relationship and teaches the caregiver to attune to the child's actual developmental level.

EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)

Ages 4+ Brain-Based Fit: Moderate With Adaptation
What It Asks the Brain to Do

EMDR asks the brain to reprocess traumatic memories that have gotten "stuck" in the emotional brain (limbic system) without being properly filed away. The therapist uses bilateral stimulation — eye movements, tapping, or sounds — while the person holds a difficult memory in mind. This appears to help the brain move memories from "active threat" storage to "past event" storage. Research shows changes in brain regions responsible for fear, memory, and regulation after EMDR treatment. The child does not need to talk extensively about the trauma.

Caregiver Involvement

Varies. For children, best practice includes caregiver involvement in preparation, stabilization, and support between sessions. The actual reprocessing is typically between the child and therapist. Some child-specific EMDR protocols include the caregiver more actively.

How to Know If It Might Be a Good Fit
  • Your child has specific traumatic memories that seem to be driving symptoms
  • Talk-based trauma processing hasn't worked because your child can't or won't narrate the experience
  • Your child can hold a memory in mind while simultaneously tracking movement or tapping (a dual-attention task)
  • The therapist has specific training in EMDR with children and neurodevelopmental adaptations
Brain-Based Consideration EMDR requires working memory — the ability to hold one thing in mind while doing another. For children with brain-based differences, this dual-attention demand may need to be carefully assessed. Adaptations include simpler language, slower pace, more caregiver involvement, and the "storytelling method" for younger developmental ages. EMDR can be effective for children with FASD who carry trauma, but it should not be a first-line standalone approach — it works best as part of a broader plan with a clinician who understands neurodevelopmental adaptations.

Somatic Experiencing (SE)

All ages Brain-Based Fit: Moderate
What It Asks the Brain to Do

Somatic Experiencing is a body-based approach to healing trauma. It works "bottom-up" — starting with the body and nervous system rather than thoughts and stories. The idea is that trauma gets "stuck" in the nervous system, and SE helps the brain complete the defensive responses (fight, flight, freeze) that got interrupted during the traumatic event. The child learns to notice and track body sensations, building the brain's capacity to regulate its own arousal. SE specifically avoids intense re-experiencing of trauma.

Caregiver Involvement

Varies. Caregivers may be included in sessions or coached on how to support regulation at home. Not as consistently caregiver-involved as some other modalities on this list.

How to Know If It Might Be a Good Fit
  • Your child has trauma-related symptoms that seem "stuck in the body" — startling, freezing, physical tension, unexplained pain
  • Talk therapy or cognitive approaches haven't worked because your child can't access or describe their experience in words
  • Your child has meltdowns or shutdowns that seem disproportionate to the trigger
  • There is a trauma history but your child can't narrate it (common in early or preverbal trauma)
Brain-Based Consideration The body-based approach bypasses the cognitive demands that are challenging for brain-based differences. For children who carry both prenatal and postnatal trauma, SE offers a way to address the nervous system directly without requiring verbal processing. However, SE does require some interoceptive awareness — the ability to notice internal body sensations — which may be variable in children with brain-based differences.
Relationship and Attachment-Based Approaches

Theraplay

Ages 0–12+ Brain-Based Fit: Strong
What It Asks the Brain to Do

Theraplay is a structured, relationship-focused play therapy where the therapist (and later the caregiver) leads playful, nurturing interactions that replicate the healthy parent-infant relationship. Unlike child-centered play therapy, Theraplay is adult-directed. It activates the social engagement system — the branch of the nervous system that supports connection rather than fight or flight. It provides the sensorimotor and co-regulation experiences that build the brain's capacity for self-regulation, and rebuilds attachment patterns through repeated experiences of attunement, playfulness, and nurture.

Caregiver Involvement

Very high. Caregivers observe sessions initially, then are coached to take over the Theraplay activities. The goal is to transform the caregiver-child interactions at home.

How to Know If It Might Be a Good Fit
  • Your child has attachment disruptions, difficulty trusting adults, or a history of relational trauma
  • Your child is withdrawn, overly controlling, or has difficulty accepting nurture or comfort
  • Your child is young or has developmental delays that make talk-based therapy difficult
  • You want to specifically strengthen the caregiver-child bond
Brain-Based Consideration Theraplay's sensorimotor, relationship-based approach works well for children whose brains respond better to "bottom-up" (body and relationship) than "top-down" (thinking and reasoning) interventions. Because it doesn't require verbal processing or insight, it's accessible to children with cognitive differences. The adult-directed structure also provides the predictability that many children with brain-based differences need.

Dyadic Developmental Psychotherapy (DDP)

Also known as: Attachment-Focused Family Therapy
Children & Teens Brain-Based Fit: Strong (especially foster/adoptive families)
What It Asks the Brain to Do

DDP creates felt safety in the therapy room so the child's brain can come out of defensive survival states (fight, flight, freeze) and move into connection and co-regulation. It is built around the attitude of PACE: Playfulness, Acceptance, Curiosity, and Empathy. The therapist uses PACE to help the child experience safety, connection, and being understood, rather than constant defensiveness. Over time, DDP helps the child's brain build new meanings about themselves and their experiences, and supports the experience of being truly known and understood by another person — which helps organize and support a developing brain.

Caregiver Involvement

Very high — caregivers are central to DDP. The child's healing is supported through the caregiver–child relationship as the therapist supports and coaches both toward safety and connection.

How to Know If It Might Be a Good Fit
  • Your child has a history of abuse, neglect, or multiple placement disruptions
  • Your child has difficulty trusting caregivers or accepting comfort
  • Your child is adopted, fostered, or in kinship care
  • Attachment and relationship are the primary concerns, not just behavior
Brain-Based Consideration DDP's relational approach is well-suited for children with brain-based differences who also have co-occurring attachment disruptions — which is common among children with FASD who have histories of foster care or adoption. The emphasis on the caregiver's inner state (not just techniques) helps caregivers manage their own frustration and grief. The verbal and narrative elements may need adaptation for children with significant cognitive, language, and processing differences.

Trust-Based Relational Intervention (TBRI)

All ages Brain-Based Fit: Very Strong (designed for this)
What It Asks the Brain to Do

TBRI is a trauma-informed, attachment-based intervention built on three principles:

  • Connecting Principles: Activate the social engagement system — build felt safety through attunement, eye contact, healthy touch, and playful engagement
  • Empowering Principles: Address the body's physiological needs that directly affect brain function — blood sugar regulation, sensory needs, hydration, and sleep. TBRI is unique in explicitly addressing the body's physical state as a driver of behavior.
  • Correcting Principles: Use "connected correction" — maintaining the relationship while setting boundaries. The brain learns it can be redirected without being rejected.
Caregiver Involvement

Extremely high. TBRI is fundamentally a caregiver-training approach. Caregivers learn all three principle sets and implement them in daily life. TBRI also trains professionals, teachers, and entire organizations.

How to Know If It Might Be a Good Fit
  • Your child comes from a "hard place" — trauma, foster care, adoption, institutional care, or prenatal substance exposure
  • Your child has attachment difficulties, sensory needs, or regulation difficulties
  • You want a comprehensive framework that addresses body, relationship, AND behavior together
  • You are adopting or fostering and want a trauma-informed parenting framework from the start
Brain-Based Consideration TBRI was designed with prenatal substance exposure explicitly in mind. The Empowering Principles directly address blood sugar dysregulation and sensory processing — issues common in FASD that are often missing from other therapies. TBRI's research notes that children with histories of prenatal substance exposure often have significant changes to insulin receptor sites, making them subject to dramatic behavioral shifts when blood sugar drops. This physiological awareness is critical for families navigating brain-based differences.
Caregiver-Focused and Problem-Solving Approaches

Collaborative and Proactive Solutions (CPS)

Also known as: Collaborative Problem Solving (former name); "The Explosive Child" approach (Ross Greene, PhD)
All ages Brain-Based Fit: Very Strong
What It Asks the Brain to Do

CPS is a non-punitive approach based on the premise that "kids do well if they can" — not "kids do well if they want to." Challenging behavior is understood as the result of lagging skills (flexibility, frustration tolerance, problem-solving) rather than lack of motivation. CPS does NOT try to motivate better behavior through rewards and consequences. Instead, it identifies the specific unsolved problems causing the behavior and teaches the child's brain to participate in solving those problems collaboratively with an adult. It uses "Plan B" conversations: an empathy step (understanding the child's concern), a define-the-adult-concern step, and an invitation to brainstorm solutions together.

Caregiver Involvement

Very high. Caregivers are taught the CPS framework and implement it at home. The caregiver shifts from trying to impose solutions ("Plan A") or giving in ("Plan C") to collaborating with the child ("Plan B").

How to Know If It Might Be a Good Fit
  • Your child has meltdowns, explosions, or extreme rigidity
  • Traditional reward and consequence approaches haven't worked — or have made things worse
  • Your child seems to struggle with flexibility and frustration tolerance, not just "won't listen"
  • You are ready to shift from a compliance-focused to a problem-solving-focused approach
Brain-Based Consideration CPS's core assumption — that challenging behavior reflects lagging skills, not lack of motivation — aligns perfectly with a brain-based understanding. For children whose brains genuinely cannot produce the flexibility or problem-solving demanded of them, CPS meets them where they are. The emphasis on solving specific, concrete problems (rather than abstract behavior goals) is an excellent fit for brain-based differences.

Parent Training Programs

Also known as: Parent Management Training (PMT), Behavioral Parent Training (BPT)
Varies by program Brain-Based Fit: Good With Adaptations
What It Asks the Brain to Do

Parent training programs teach caregivers specific skills to change the environment around a child's challenging behavior. The core idea: the caregiver is the primary agent of change. These programs restructure the environment so the child's brain gets clear, predictable signals (reducing confusion and anxiety), positive behavior gets noticed and reinforced (strengthening those neural pathways), and the caregiver-child relationship improves. They work primarily through environmental change and repetition — not by asking the child to think differently.

Caregiver Involvement

The caregiver IS the client. The therapist works mainly with the caregiver, who then implements skills at home. This is the defining feature of all parent training programs.

Named Programs Under This Umbrella
  • Parent Management Training (PMT / Kazdin Model) — Ages up to 12. The "classic" parent training: parents learn skills through role-play, then practice at home. Typically 8–16 weeks.
  • Parent Management Training – Oregon Model (PMTO) — Ages 3–16. Focuses on the "coercion cycle" — how parent-child interactions can accidentally reinforce escalating behavior. Teaches five core practices.
  • Parent-Child Interaction Therapy (PCIT) — Ages 2–7. The therapist coaches the caregiver in real time via an earpiece while the caregiver plays with the child. Builds the relationship first, then adds structure. Mastery-based — families move at their own pace.
  • Triple P (Positive Parenting Program) — Birth to 16. A tiered system with five levels of intensity, from light-touch prevention to intensive family intervention.
  • The Incredible Years — Infant through age 12. Group-based program using video modeling and group discussion. Separate curricula for parents, children, and teachers.
  • Helping the Noncompliant Child (HNC) — Ages 3–8. Two-phase approach: build the positive relationship first, then add clear instructions and follow-through. Mastery-based.
  • Defiant Children / Defiant Teens (Barkley) — Structured parent training with a strong focus on ADHD-related defiance and executive function challenges.
FASD-Specific Parent Training Programs
  • Families Moving Forward (FMF) — Specifically designed for FASD. Focuses on changing the environment and caregiver expectations rather than changing the child. Teaches caregivers to reframe behavior as brain-based rather than intentional. Includes school consultation and community connections. This is the most FASD-specific parent training program available.
  • Parents and Children Together (PACT) — Also designed for FASD. Uses the "How is your engine running?" metaphor to teach about arousal and regulation states. Addresses behavior regulation, executive functioning, and parent effectiveness.
How to Know If It Might Be a Good Fit
  • Your child's behavior is the primary concern and you want concrete skills to handle it
  • You want to be the one doing the work, not waiting for a therapist to "fix" your child
  • You want peer support from other caregivers (group-based programs like Incredible Years)
  • For FASD specifically: ask whether the program has been adapted for brain-based differences, or look for FMF/PACT
Brain-Based Consideration The environmental and behavioral approach is a strength for brain-based differences — these programs change the world around the child rather than expecting the child to change. However, standard parent training programs may need adaptation: expectations for speed of behavior change should be adjusted, reward systems may need to be simpler and more concrete, and caregivers need to understand the brain-based reasons for behavior — not just label it as "noncompliance." Programs designed specifically for FASD (like Families Moving Forward) have this understanding built in.
