For Self-Advocates

People living with FASD and brain-based differences

You know those things people say you should "just" do? Just focus. Just remember. Just calm down. Just try harder.

Maybe you are trying. Harder than anyone sees. And it's still not working.

Your brain works differently. That's not an excuse. It's neuroscience.

What You Might Be Experiencing

Being Misunderstood

Being blamed for things your brain makes harder. Feeling like something is "wrong" with you when really the world isn't built for your brain.

Struggling with "Easy" Things

Things that seem easy for others — keeping track of time, remembering instructions, managing emotions — can take enormous effort.

Not Knowing How to Ask

You may not know the difference between treatment types or how to tell a provider what you need. People may expect too much — or not enough.

What the Research Actually Says

FASD is a brain-based disability caused by prenatal alcohol exposure. It affects memory, attention, executive functioning, impulse control, sensory processing, and emotional regulation. These are neurological differences — not character flaws.

Research shows that people with FASD are at higher risk for mental health challenges, justice involvement, unstable housing, and employment difficulties — not because of who they are, but because the systems around them weren't built for how their brain works.

The same research shows that the right supports genuinely change outcomes. Stable relationships, accurate diagnosis, and environments that fit your brain are among the strongest protective factors identified in the literature.

Your Brain

Things that are often hard for people with FASD and other brain-based differences:

  • Memory — especially without visual or written reminders
  • Time — planning ahead, knowing how long things take
  • Cause and effect — connecting current actions to future consequences
  • Emotional regulation — feelings arrive fast and feel big
  • Transitions — unexpected changes are especially hard
  • Sensory overload — sounds, lights, textures
  • Social rules — reading unspoken expectations

Knowing your patterns is powerful. Notice when you function best. Identify what environments help or hinder you.

What Can Actually Help

Stable Relationships

People who know you, believe you, and stay consistent. This is one of the strongest protective factors identified in the research.

Accurate Diagnosis

Understanding what's actually going on in your brain changes everything — from the kind of therapy you get to how you see yourself.

The Right Environment

Structure, visual supports, reduced sensory load, and people who understand brain-based differences make daily life work better.

What You Can Ask For

Accommodations aren't special treatment. They're adjustments that let your brain do what it's capable of.

  • Under the ADA, you have the right to request reasonable accommodations in work and public settings
  • Under IDEA and Section 504, you have rights in educational settings
  • You don't have to disclose everything — you just have to communicate what you need

Talking to People in Your Life

Talking to Providers

You can tell them: "I need things explained simply." "I need written instructions." "I need more time." A good provider will work with you, not just talk at you.

Talking to Employers

You can ask for accommodations like written task lists, quiet workspace, check-ins, or flexible scheduling. You have the right under the ADA.

Talking to Educators

Ask for extra time, written instructions, a quiet testing space, or a point person you can go to when you're overwhelmed.

Talking to Friends

You get to decide how much you share. But letting people know what helps — like texting instead of calling, or being patient with memory — can strengthen relationships.

Practical Tips

  • Use alarms and reminders for everything — there's no shame in external support
  • Keep a written or visual schedule where you can see it
  • When you're overwhelmed, take a break before trying to solve the problem
  • Find one person you trust who can help you think through hard decisions
  • Learn what sensory inputs help you regulate (music, movement, quiet, texture)
