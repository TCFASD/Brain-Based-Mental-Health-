For Caregivers

You are not failing. You are parenting a brain that needs a different kind of support.

Caring for someone with FASD or other brain-based differences is fundamentally different from typical parenting or caregiving. Traditional approaches often fail because they assume typical brain functioning — and when the brain works differently, different support is needed.

Common Challenges You Might Be Facing

Strategies Fall Short

Strategies that worked with other children don't work here. Consequences, rewards, and reasoning don't land the way they should.

Professionals Don't Understand

Providers who don't understand FASD or brain-based differences may recommend approaches that make things worse.

Feeling Blamed

You may feel blamed for your child's struggles while constantly needing to advocate for them across every system.

What Brain-Based Care Actually Looks Like

  • Adjust expectations to match brain age, not chronological age. A 14-year-old with FASD may have the emotional regulation capacity of a much younger child. That's not defiance — it's neurobiology.
  • Build structure into the environment, not just into the child. External scaffolding like visual schedules, routines, reminders, and caregiver support do the job that executive functioning can't yet do on its own.
  • Use immediate, concrete responses instead of delayed consequences. "You lost your screen time for the week" often doesn't connect for a brain that struggles with cause-and-effect across time. "Right now, we're taking a break because of what just happened" is more likely to land.
  • Expect slower generalization. Skills learned in one setting may not show up in another without support. Repetition across contexts, with your involvement, is how it transfers.
  • Take care of yourself. You can't regulate a dysregulated child from an empty tank. Caregiver burnout is real, and your needs matter in this equation.

A Note on the Grief

Caregiving for someone with brain-based differences often involves a kind of grief that doesn't have a name. Grief for the milestones that may not come on schedule. Grief for the relationships that don't understand. Grief for the version of family life you expected.

That grief is real, and it can coexist with deep love and fierce advocacy. You don't have to choose between them.

Working with Your Treatment Team

Research consistently shows that treatment outcomes for children with FASD and other brain-based differences are significantly better when caregivers are active partners in the process. The family is part of the intervention.

Share What You See

Share what you observe at home, specifically. You see patterns that providers don't.

Ask How It Connects

Ask how the work in therapy connects to home. Name the mismatch when it happens.

Find the Right Fit

It's okay to find a different provider if the fit doesn't work. The right partnership matters.

Practical Tips

  • Visual support — a picture schedule, a checklist, a written rule on the wall
  • Talk less — fewer words land better when the brain is overwhelmed
  • Give transition warnings before shifts in activity
  • Celebrate small things specifically — not just "good job" but exactly what they did
  • When something isn't working, change the environment or expectation — not the child
  • Find other caregivers who get it — isolation makes everything harder

Your Rights as a Caregiver

When you walk into your child's therapist's office, you are not just a bystander — you are a partner in their care. Federal law and professional ethics codes give you clear rights in the mental health treatment setting, including:

  • Informed consent — to know what treatment is being recommended, why, and what the alternatives are before it begins
  • Know the therapy type — to ask what approach is being used and whether there is evidence it works for your child's needs
  • Participate in treatment planning — to be involved in setting goals and making decisions, not kept out of the process
  • Access your child's records — to request and review treatment records under HIPAA
  • Ask about qualifications — to know the therapist's training and experience with your child's specific condition
  • Confidentiality protections — to control who your child's information is shared with
  • Request a different provider — to change therapists if the fit isn't right, without guilt
  • Get a second opinion — to seek another professional's perspective at any time
  • Understand costs and insurance — to get clear billing information and equitable mental health coverage
  • Refuse or discontinue treatment — to stop therapy if it isn't working for your family
  • Discharge planning — to be involved when therapy is ending, with referrals and a plan for what's next
