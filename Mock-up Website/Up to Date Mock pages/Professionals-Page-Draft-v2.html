For Mental Health Professionals

Your training gave you strong tools. But when the brain works differently, those tools need a different starting point.

When Nothing Works

You are a competent clinician with solid training and evidence-based interventions. But your go-to approaches are not landing with certain clients, and neurobiology might be why.

Most therapeutic models were built around typical brain functioning. They have real limits when the brain works differently. That might look like:

  • CBT falling flat because cause-and-effect reasoning is impaired
  • DBT skills that do not generalize due to executive functioning limitations
  • Trauma processing that stalls
  • Behavioral plans that backfire when consequences feel arbitrary

The issue is not your competence. The issue is that your training likely did not teach you to assess and understand the brain before choosing how to treat.

Brain-Based Practice: From Understanding to Action

A guided learning journey that takes you from understanding why brain-based practice matters to confidently adapting your clinical work. Each step builds on the last and includes everything you need to learn, reflect, apply, and check your progress.

You do not need to complete every step before helping clients. Even the first step will shift how you see and respond to complexity.

Planning with the Brain in Mind

Building treatment plans that match actual brain capacity

Most treatment plans are built around goals the client should reach. Brain-based treatment planning starts with an honest look at what the client's brain can actually do right now. This step teaches you to design strategies matched to neurobiological capacity, involve caregivers as environmental supports, and set goals around healthy interdependence rather than pushing for independence the brain cannot sustain.

Adapting Your Approach

Making your existing modalities work for different brains

CBT, DBT, TBRI, PCIT are not wrong for clients with brain-based differences. They often need to be adapted from the inside out. This step teaches you to ask: what does this intervention assume about the brain, and does this brain meet those assumptions? If not, what needs to change? At the advanced level, this happens in real time during sessions.

Frequently Asked Questions

I do not work with FASD specifically. Is this still relevant?
Yes. The brain-based framework applies to any client whose brain works differently, including autism, ADHD, intellectual disabilities, learning differences, traumatic brain injury, and complex trauma. FASD is a primary focus because it is one of the most commonly missed conditions, but the clinical skills transfer broadly.
How does this relate to trauma-informed care?
Brain-based practice builds on and extends trauma-informed care. Trauma-informed care changed the question from "what is wrong with you" to "what happened to you." Brain-based practice adds "how does your brain process what happened, and what does it need now?" The two frameworks are complementary.
Will this work with my therapeutic approach?
This does not replace your existing modalities. It helps you adapt what you already use, whether that is CBT, DBT, EMDR, play therapy, or another approach, so it fits the neurobiological reality of the client in front of you.
Do I need to go through every step in order?
Not necessarily. The self-assessment helps you identify where you already are. If you have strong foundational knowledge but struggle with treatment planning, you can focus there. The steps build on each other, but your development will not be uniform across all of them, and that is normal.
Is there certification or CE available?
Training through The Florida Center for Early Childhood offers continuing education credits. Visit the training calendar on the Education page for current offerings and CE details.
