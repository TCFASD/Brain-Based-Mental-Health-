Tips for Creating a Brain-Based Mental Health Treatment Plan

A guide for mental health clinicians working with brain-based differences
1

Include Caregivers and Supportive Adults

While the individual is the primary client, involving caregivers and supportive adults in treatment can make a significant difference. Mental health challenges often stem from environments that do not accommodate brain-based differences. Equipping the people around the client helps create a more adaptive environment and reduces symptoms. This applies at all stages of life, including adulthood.

2

Focus on Interdependence, Not Just Independence

The goal of treatment is to create an environment of interdependency versus independency. It is important for brain-based informed mental health clinicians to reassess the concepts of "dependency" and "enabling" within treatment and systems.

3

Support Caregivers Too

Recognize that caregivers also may have their own brain-based differences and challenges. Set realistic, brain-friendly goals for caregivers to ensure they are supported in their role.

4

Match Learning Styles

Assess the learning styles of everyone involved: the client, caregivers, and support providers like teachers. Deliver information in the format that works best for each person, whether that is videos, written materials, hands-on practice, or verbal explanations.

5

Build Trust First

Individuals with brain-based differences may have also experienced trauma that affects how they trust and connect. Before meaningful engagement can happen, a sense of felt safety must be established with both the clinician and supportive adults. Trust is the foundation that everything else is built on.

6

Consider the Environment

The environment significantly influences the therapeutic process. Consider lighting, colors, clutter, and transitions in the therapy office and across other settings like home, school, and work. A well-designed environment promotes regulation, reduces stress, and supports skill development.

7

Use Strengths to Guide Treatment Goals

  • Base goals on the client's brain strengths and challenges
  • Center the client's voice in the goal-setting process
  • Build on natural abilities and interests, not just areas for improvement
  • Align objectives with cognitive and emotional development, not chronological age
  • Expect that skills may not transfer across settings. Involve the full support system for consistency
  • Adjust the pace. Break goals into manageable steps. Keep them realistic, outcome-oriented, and open-ended rather than time-restricted
