Your Rights as a Caregiver in Mental Health Treatment

You are a partner in your child's care — and you have rights.

1. The Right to Informed Consent

Before therapy begins, the therapist must explain what treatment they are recommending, why, what it involves, the risks and benefits, and what alternatives exist. You must agree before treatment starts. If the approach changes, you should be informed again.

"Can you walk me through what this treatment involves and why you're recommending it?"
APA Ethics Code, Standards 3.10 & 10.01 • NASW Code of Ethics, Section 1.03

2. The Right to Know the Therapy Type

You can ask what type of therapy is being used (CBT, play therapy, EMDR, DBT, etc.), why it was chosen, and whether there is research supporting it for your child's needs. If a therapist cannot explain what they are doing, that is a concern.

"What approach are you using, and what does the research say about it for kids with brain-based differences?"
APA Ethics Code, Standard 10.01(a) • APA Evidence-Based Practice Policy

3. The Right to Participate in Treatment Planning

You have the right to understand treatment goals, provide input about what is and is not working, and be part of decisions about changes. You bring essential knowledge about your child's history, daily life, and what has been tried before.

"I'd like to understand the treatment goals and share what I'm seeing at home."
APA Ethics Code, Standard 10.01 • SAMHSA Family-Driven Care Guidelines

4. The Right to Access Treatment Records

Under HIPAA, as a parent or guardian you generally have the right to see your child's mental health records. A therapist cannot simply refuse because "it's confidential." You can request copies, and the provider must respond within 30 days.

"I'd like to request a copy of my child's treatment records."
HIPAA Privacy Rule, 45 CFR § 164.502(g) & 164.524

5. The Right to Ask About Qualifications

You can ask about a therapist's degree, license, training, and experience — especially with your child's specific condition. The therapist must answer honestly. You can also verify any license through your state's licensing board (public records).

"Can you tell me about your experience with kids who have brain-based differences like FASD?"
APA Ethics Code, Standards 2.01 & 5.01 • NASW Code of Ethics, Section 4.06

6. The Right to Confidentiality Protections

Your child's mental health information is protected. The therapist cannot share it without your authorization. You have the right to know who has access and to request a log of all disclosures made.

"Who has access to my child's treatment information?"
HIPAA Privacy Rule, 45 CFR Parts 160 & 164

7. The Right to Request a Different Provider

You are never locked in to a specific therapist. If the fit is not right, you can request a different provider at any time. The therapist has an ethical obligation to help with the transition and provide referrals — not to make you feel guilty for leaving.

"I think we need a provider who is a better fit. Can you help with a referral?"
APA Ethics Code, Standards 3.12 & 10.10 • NASW Code of Ethics, Section 1.16

8. The Right to a Second Opinion

You can seek evaluation or consultation from another professional at any time — about a diagnosis, treatment recommendation, or any clinical question. The current therapist cannot prevent this.

"I'd like to get a second opinion. Can you share the relevant records with the provider I choose?"
APA Ethics Code, Standard 10.04 • General principle of patient autonomy

9. The Right to Understand Billing and Insurance

Under the Mental Health Parity Act, insurance cannot impose stricter limits on mental health than on medical care. Co-pays, visit limits, and prior authorization must be comparable. Under the No Surprises Act, you can request a Good Faith Estimate of costs before treatment begins.

"Can you provide a Good Faith Estimate of costs?"
Mental Health Parity Act (MHPAEA) • No Surprises Act

10. The Right to Refuse or Discontinue Treatment

You can stop your child's therapy at any time. Consent is voluntary and can be withdrawn. A therapist should never use guilt or threats to keep you in treatment. (Exception: court-ordered treatment may limit this right.)

"We've decided to pause treatment. Can you provide a summary of the work so far?"
Troxel v. Granville (2000) • APA Ethics Code, Standard 3.10(b)

11. The Right to Be Involved in Discharge and Transition Planning

When therapy is ending, you have the right to advance notice, a summary of progress, recommendations for ongoing support, and referrals to other providers. A therapist should not abruptly end services without giving you time to plan for what comes next.

"Before we wrap up, I'd like to discuss progress, what to watch for, and whether my child needs follow-up services."
APA Ethics Code, Standard 10.10 • NASW Code of Ethics, Section 1.16 • ACA Code of Ethics, Section A.11
A Note About State Laws: Mental health law varies by state. The age at which a minor can consent to their own treatment ranges from 12 to 16 depending on where you live. The rights here are grounded in federal law and national professional ethics codes, but check your state's specific laws as well. This is for educational purposes and is not legal advice.
