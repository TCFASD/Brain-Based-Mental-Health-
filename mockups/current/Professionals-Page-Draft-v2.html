For Clinicians

Your training gave you strong tools. But when the brain works differently, those tools need a different starting point.

Who Is This For?

This site is built for clinicians working with clients of all ages who have brain-based differences, including:

  • Clients with FASD or suspected prenatal alcohol exposure
  • Neurodivergent clients (autism, ADHD, intellectual and learning differences)
  • Complex developmental trauma and attachment disruption
  • Clients who have been labeled "treatment-resistant"
  • Anyone whose presentation does not match standard treatment expectations

Brain-Based Mental Health Treatment

Brain-based practice is not a separate, manualized treatment. It is a clinical orientation that can be added to existing models like CBT, DBT, TF-CBT, EMDR, PCIT, DDP, CPS, and play therapy. It assumes collaboration with medical and developmental professionals when needed, and emphasizes adapting your practice rather than making diagnoses.

Brain-based practice asks for neurodevelopmental humility, recognizing that capacity, not just motivation, shapes what clients can do. It draws on evidence about FASD, complex trauma, and neurodevelopmental conditions, consistent with trauma-informed and neurodiversity-affirming frameworks.

It does not replace the need for diagnosis when indicated, nor does it override clinical or ethical guidelines. It is a lens to help clinicians adapt evidence-based practices to clients whose brains work differently.

Why This Matters

Before learning new frameworks or tools, you need to understand why what you were trained to do is not always landing with certain clients. This section names that gap and why brain-based practice closes it.

You are a competent clinician with solid training and evidence-based interventions. But your go-to approaches are not landing with certain clients, and neurobiology might be why.

Most therapeutic models were built around typical brain functioning and may need adaptation when brain-based differences are present. That might look like:

  • CBT may fall flat when abstract reasoning or generalization are affected, requiring adaptation
  • DBT skills may not generalize easily without repetition and environmental support
  • Trauma processing that stalls
  • Behavioral plans may backfire when consequences feel arbitrary, abstract, or delayed

The issue is not your competence. The issue is that your training likely did not teach you to assess and understand the brain before choosing how to treat.

Your training remains valuable. This is about adding a neurodevelopmental lens that many standard models were not explicitly designed to highlight.

Brain-based practice is part of a broader shift in how clinicians understand the people they serve. It sits alongside trauma-informed care, cultural humility, and neurodiversity-affirming practice. Together, these frameworks ask clinicians to expand their identity, not just their toolkit.

Brain-Based Practice: From Understanding to Action

A guided learning journey that takes you from understanding why brain-based practice matters to confidently adapting your clinical work. Each step builds on the last and includes everything you need to learn, reflect, apply, and check your progress.

You do not need to complete every step before helping clients. Even the first step will shift how you see and respond to complexity.

What You Will Gain

  • Clinical confidence with clients who used to feel impossible
  • Real-time adaptation skills that change how you show up in session
  • Reduced frustration and burnout from cases that used to stall
  • A professional community who shares your language

Frequently Asked Questions

I do not work with FASD specifically. Is this still relevant?
Yes. Because FASD is often underdiagnosed â€” estimates suggest as many as 1 in 20 people may be affected â€” you are likely already working with clients whose brains may have been shaped by prenatal alcohol exposure that has not been identified. Beyond FASD, the brain-based framework applies to any client whose brain works differently, including autism, ADHD, intellectual disabilities, learning differences, traumatic brain injury, and complex trauma. The clinical skills you build here transfer broadly.
How does this relate to trauma-informed care?
Brain-based practice builds on and extends trauma-informed care. Trauma-informed care changed the question from "what is wrong with you" to "what happened to you." Brain-based practice adds "how does your brain process what happened, and what does it need now?" The two frameworks are complementary.
Will this work with my therapeutic approach?
This does not replace your existing modalities. It helps you adapt what you already use, whether that is CBT, DBT, EMDR, play therapy, or another approach, so it fits the neurobiological reality of the client in front of you.
Do I need to go through every step in order?
Not necessarily. The self-assessment helps you identify where you already are. The steps build on each other, but your development will not be uniform across all of them, and that is normal.
