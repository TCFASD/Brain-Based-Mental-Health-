Dyadic Developmental Psychotherapy (DDP) Feedback

Also known as: Attachment-Focused Family Therapy
Children & Teens | Brain-Based Fit: Strong (especially foster/adoptive families)
What It Asks the Brain to Do:

DDP creates felt safety in the therapy room so the child's brain can come out of defensive mode. It is built around the attitude of PACE: Playfulness, Acceptance, Curiosity, and Empathy. The therapist uses PACE to activate the social engagement system (safety, connection) instead of the defensive system (fight, flight, freeze). Over time, DDP helps the child's brain create new narratives about themselves and their experiences, and builds the experience of being truly known and understood by another person — which organizes the developing brain.

Caregiver Involvement:

Very high — the caregiver is in every session. DDP works to heal the child through the caregiver-child relationship, with the therapist supporting both.

How to Know If It Might Be a Good Fit:
  • Your child has a history of abuse, neglect, or multiple placement disruptions
  • Your child has difficulty trusting caregivers or accepting comfort
  • Your child is adopted, fostered, or in kinship care
  • Attachment and relationship are the primary concerns, not just behavior
Brain-Based Consideration: DDP's relational approach is well-suited for children with brain-based differences who also have co-occurring attachment disruptions — which is common among children with FASD who have histories of foster care or adoption. The emphasis on the caregiver's inner state (not just techniques) helps caregivers manage their own frustration and grief. The verbal and narrative elements may need adaptation for children with significant cognitive differences.
