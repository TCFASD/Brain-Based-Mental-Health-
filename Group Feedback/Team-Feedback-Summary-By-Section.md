## INDIVIDUAL THERAPY MODALITIES

### CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy)
- Accurate; characterization as "Limited Without Modification" reflects reliance on abstract thinking, working memory, generalization [Jessica W.]
- Add: CBT often appears successful in-session but fails in real-world application due to deficits in generalization
- Caregivers should look for therapists who use heavily modified, concrete, repetitive CBT or integrate experiential/environmental supports
- When CBT "doesn't work," it's frequently misinterpreted as noncompliance — which can be harmful to the child and family [Jessica W.]

### DBT (Dialectical Behavior Therapy)
- Accurate; "moderate fit" framing and emphasis on body-based distress tolerance is right [Jessica W.]
- Full-model DBT is often too cognitively/logistically demanding for FASD families unless significantly adapted
- Most useful components: distress tolerance (especially sensory/body-based) and simplified emotion regulation skill-building
- Explicitly suggest caregivers seek skills-based or adapted DBT rather than full-protocol adherence
- Reinforcement, repetition, and caregiver modeling are essential for skill acquisition [Jessica W.]

### TF-CBT (Trauma-Focused CBT)
- Accurate; "moderate with adaptation" framing and emphasis on caregiver involvement as a major strength is right [Jessica W., T. Eller]
- The trauma narrative component can be particularly challenging for individuals with FASD due to memory gaps, sequencing difficulties, language limitations [Jessica W.]
- Caregivers should ensure clinician is comfortable adapting narrative work (visual, experiential, or co-constructed formats) and pacing (often significantly slower) [Jessica W.]
- Stabilization and regulation often need to take much longer before trauma processing begins [Jessica W.]
- Help caregivers discern what is trauma response vs. brain-based difference — needs to occur in psychoeducation; sets realistic expectations for what may/may not change [T. Eller]
- Heavy cognitive coping component — caregiver must be actively involved in reframing, connecting feeling/thought/behavior [T. Eller]
- Both caregiver and clinician must be aware of **confabulation** during the trauma narrative [T. Eller]
- Relaxation and affective modulation will need additional support, including sensory work [T. Eller]
- Spend time enhancing safety given generalization difficulties — needs caregiver coaching to move out of the office into real life, may need repeating as the individual hits new milestones [T. Eller]

### DDP (Dyadic Developmental Psychotherapy)
- Generally accurate but needs clinical refinement [Janessa]
- Rewording suggestion: "DDP creates felt safety in the therapy room so the child's brain can come out of **defensive survival states** (fight, flight, freeze) and move into connection and co-regulation. The therapist uses PACE (Playfulness, Acceptance, Curiosity, Empathy) to help the child experience safety, connection, and being understood…" [Janessa]
- Reframe caregiver role: "caregivers are central to DDP" — healing is supported through the caregiver–child relationship as the therapist supports/coaches both toward safety and connection [Janessa]
- Add "Challenges and Adaptations" section [Janessa]:
  - Cognitive, language, and processing differences impact PACE delivery
  - PACE relies on co-regulation; social-cognition challenges can make synchronized, shared-experience interactions difficult
  - Intersubjectivity is demanding (joint attention, theory of mind, reading social cues)
  - Narrative work limited by memory, sequencing, readiness to use emotional language
  - Reflection inconsistent when cause-and-effect reasoning or working memory is impaired
  - Pacing may feel too fast — use less language, more pauses, concrete anchors (photos, objects, simple storylines)
  - Caregiver training is key — PACE is a relational stance by the caregiver/clinician; cognitive load shifts off the child onto the regulated, coached adult

### Play Therapy
- Accurate [E. Goodman]
- Add: Play therapy accesses the right side of the brain (art, music, expression, color) while traditional talk therapy considers only the left side (language, math, organization, black-and-white) [E. Goodman]
- The brain grows right-to-left and bottom-to-top — play therapy is perfect for children, especially those showing dysmaturity in various areas (emotional/developmental age lags or exceeds chronological age) [E. Goodman]
- Effective for older children, teens, and even adults when implemented sensitively, due to **neurointegration** — processing through both sides of the brain [E. Goodman]
- Extremely effective when a "front door" approach doesn't work [E. Goodman]

### TBRI (Trust-Based Relational Intervention)
- Accurate [E. Goodman, Tami Eller]
- TBRI is a connected-style alternative to traditional, punitive parenting; useful for all relationships but especially beneficial for the populations listed [E. Goodman]
- TBRI is not limited to specific populations but is exceptionally beneficial for those listed [E. Goodman]
- **Be careful with expectations around Correcting Principles** — youth may not be able to participate in a "redo" [Tami Eller]
- Sometimes the ability to "correct" the behavior is variable [Tami Eller]
- May need to adjust expectations and go backward to build forward, depending on the day or time of day [Tami Eller]
- May take more "redo" and "correcting" cycles for pro-social behaviors to be learned [Tami Eller]

### ABA (Applied Behavior Analysis) — New Content Submission [Sydney and Emma]
Full proposed content for adding ABA to the therapy guide:
- **Ages**: 2–18 (traditionally), through adulthood
- **Brain-Based Fit**: Low to Moderate (highly dependent on approach and adaptation)
- **What it asks the brain to do**: Structured, behavior-based; teaches skills through repetition, reinforcement, breaking tasks into small steps; learning through external cues, rewards, consistent practice — not internal insight or emotional processing
- **Caregiver involvement**: Caregiver training and home carryover are key; strong involvement improves generalization and consistency
- **Good fit if**: Child benefits from repetition, predictability, clear routines; learns best through structured teaching and reinforcement; communication, daily living, safety, behavior reduction, social skills are primary goals
- **Good provider fit if**: Provider includes/coaches caregivers; emphasizes relationship, regulation, adapting to the child's brain; uses flexible, child-centered, non-compliance-driven approach
- **Brain-Based Consideration**: Helpful for specific skills when adapted to be relationship-based and regulation-focused; traditional models can place high demands on attention/compliance/behavioral control — challenging for FASD, trauma, ADHD; if not adapted, may increase stress or mask underlying needs; a neurodevelopmentally-informed provider prioritizing flexibility, connection, and sensory/emotional needs is key

## SUPPLEMENTAL: ADAPTATION MODELS [L. Gumbiner]

Two models from Delage et al., 2026 Seattle FASD Conference, submitted as supplemental content:

### Clinician-Driven Model (7 steps)
1. Begin with listening and relationship-building
2. Identify accessibility barriers in the existing intervention
3. Preserve core treatment elements
4. Modify delivery for neurodevelopmental fit
5. Adjust expectations and measurement of progress
6. Build peer and community connection when possible
7. Engage in ongoing reflection and consultation

### Research-Driven Model (7 steps)
1. Co-define the problem and priorities
2. Multi-informant needs assessment
3. Ground adaptations in theory and living experience (EPIS framework)
4. Systematic adaptation design and documentation (MADI framework)
5. Workforce preparation and collaborative training
6. Feasibility trial with continuous feedback loops
7. Iterative refinement and sustainment planning
