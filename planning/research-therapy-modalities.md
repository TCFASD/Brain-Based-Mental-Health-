## PART 1: THE PARENT TRAINING FAMILY

### Umbrella Terms
The field uses several interchangeable umbrella terms:
- **Parent Management Training (PMT)** -- the most common academic term
- **Behavioral Parent Training (BPT)** -- emphasizes the behavioral learning theory roots
- **Parent Training** -- the broadest, most casual term

All of these refer to a family of programs that teach caregivers specific skills to change the dynamics around a child's challenging behavior. The core idea across all of them: **the caregiver is the primary agent of change.** The therapist works mainly with the caregiver, not the child.

### What All Parent Training Programs Ask the Brain to Do
These programs work on the behavioral/reinforcement level. They restructure the environment around the child so that:
- The child's brain gets **clear, predictable signals** (reducing confusion and anxiety)
- Positive behavior gets **noticed and reinforced** (strengthening those neural pathways)
- Challenging behavior gets **consistent, calm consequences** (weakening those pathways over time)
- The caregiver-child relationship improves (building the attachment foundation that helps the child's brain feel safe)

**Key brain principle:** These approaches work primarily through **environmental change and repetition** rather than asking the child to think differently. This makes them more accessible for children with brain-based differences who may struggle with insight-based therapies.

### Named Programs Under the Parent Training Umbrella

#### 1. Parent Management Training (PMT) -- Kazdin Model
- **What it is:** The "classic" parent training. Parents meet with a therapist (usually without the child present) and learn skills through role-play, then practice at home.
- **Ages:** Up to age 12
- **Format:** Individual family sessions, typically 8-16 weeks
- **What it asks the brain to do:** Parents learn to give clear instructions, use positive reinforcement (praise, token systems), and apply consistent consequences. The child's brain learns through repeated experience that positive behavior "works" and gets rewarded.
- **Caregiver involvement:** Very high -- the caregiver IS the client. The therapist trains the caregiver, who then implements skills at home.
- **Best for:** Children with oppositional/defiant behavior, conduct problems, aggression. Works well when the parent-child relationship has some positive foundation already but behavior is the immediate problem.
- **FASD note:** The environmental/behavioral approach is a strength for FASD families. However, standard PMT may need adaptation: expectations for speed of behavior change should be adjusted, token/reward systems may need to be simpler and more concrete, and caregivers need psychoeducation about brain-based reasons for behavior (not just "noncompliance").

#### 2. Parent Management Training -- Oregon Model (PMTO)
- **What it is:** Developed by Gerald Patterson and colleagues at the Oregon Social Learning Center. Considered one of the foundational PMT programs. Emphasizes the "coercion cycle" -- how parent-child interactions can accidentally reinforce escalating behavior.
- **Ages:** 3-16
- **Format:** Individual family sessions or group format, 14-30+ sessions
- **What it asks the brain to do:** Breaks the coercion cycle by teaching caregivers five core practices: skill encouragement, positive involvement, problem solving, monitoring/supervision, and effective discipline. The child's brain gradually learns that escalation doesn't produce the desired result, while positive approaches do.
- **Caregiver involvement:** Very high -- caregiver is the primary client.
- **Best for:** Families where there are escalating conflict patterns. Strong evidence base across cultures.
- **FASD note:** The focus on understanding interaction patterns is valuable. Caregivers need to understand that a child with FASD isn't choosing to escalate -- their brain may genuinely struggle with the executive function needed to de-escalate.

#### 3. Parent-Child Interaction Therapy (PCIT)
- **What it is:** The therapist coaches the caregiver in real time via an earpiece while the caregiver interacts with the child. Developed by Sheila Eyberg.
- **Ages:** 2-7 (primary range)
- **Format:** Individual family sessions, 14-17 sessions on average, mastery-based (not time-limited)
- **What it asks the brain to do:** Two phases build the relationship first, then add structure:
  - **Phase 1 (Child-Directed Interaction):** The caregiver follows the child's lead in play, using PRIDE skills (Praise, Reflect, Imitate, Describe, Enthusiasm). This floods the child's brain with felt safety and positive attention, building the attachment foundation.
  - **Phase 2 (Parent-Directed Interaction):** The caregiver learns to give clear, specific commands and follow through with consistent consequences. The child's brain learns predictability and structure.
- **Caregiver involvement:** Extremely high -- caregiver is actively coached during every session with the child present.
- **Best for:** Young children with tantrums, aggression, defiance, and/or attachment disruptions. Especially strong when the parent-child relationship itself needs repair. Has trauma-informed adaptations (PCIT with trauma protocol).
- **FASD note:** The live coaching is a significant strength -- caregivers get immediate feedback rather than trying to remember skills from a session. The relationship-first approach (Phase 1) is critical for FASD families. The mastery-based progression (families move at their own pace) is also a better fit than rigid timelines.

#### 4. Triple P (Positive Parenting Program)
- **What it is:** Developed in Australia by Matt Sanders. A multi-level system with five tiers of intervention intensity, from universal media campaigns (Level 1) to intensive family intervention (Level 5).
- **Ages:** Birth to 16
- **Format:** Varies by level -- can be self-directed, brief consultation, group, individual, or intensive. Level 4 (standard) is 8-10 sessions.
- **What it asks the brain to do:** Similar to other PMT programs at its core -- teaches caregivers to create positive environments, use constructive approaches, and set consistent boundaries. The tiered system means the level of brain-change work matches the level of need.
- **Caregiver involvement:** High, but varies by level. At higher levels, caregivers receive individual coaching.
- **Best for:** Flexible -- can work for mild to severe behavior problems depending on the tier. The lower tiers are good prevention; the higher tiers are treatment. Widely available.
- **FASD note:** The tiered approach can be useful, but families of children with FASD will almost certainly need Level 4 or 5 (the more intensive, individualized tiers). Generic group formats at lower levels may frustrate FASD families if the content doesn't account for brain-based differences.

#### 5. The Incredible Years
- **What it is:** Developed by Carolyn Webster-Stratton. A group-based program with separate curricula for parents, children, and teachers. Uses video modeling, group discussion, and practice.
- **Ages:** Infant through age 12, broken into four age groups (Baby, Toddler, Preschool, School-Age)
- **Format:** Group sessions, 12-20 weeks depending on version
- **What it asks the brain to do:** Uses social learning theory -- caregivers learn by watching video examples and practicing together. Children's groups work on emotional literacy, problem-solving, and social skills. The multi-component approach means the child's brain is being supported from multiple angles (home, school, peer group).
- **Caregiver involvement:** High -- caregivers attend weekly group sessions and practice at home. A key strength is the peer support aspect of the group format.
- **Best for:** Families who benefit from group support and learning from other parents. Strong evidence for preventing and treating conduct problems.
- **FASD note:** The group format can be a double-edged sword -- peer support is valuable, but parents of children with FASD may feel their situation is different from parents of neurotypical children with behavior problems. The child social skills group may need adaptation for children whose brains process social information differently.

#### 6. Helping the Noncompliant Child (HNC)
- **What it is:** Developed by Rex Forehand and Robert McMahon. A clinic-based, individual family program with two clear phases.
- **Ages:** 3-8
- **Format:** Individual family sessions, typically 8-12 sessions, mastery-based
- **What it asks the brain to do:**
  - **Phase 1:** Parents learn Attends (verbal descriptions of child's behavior), Rewards, and strategic Ignoring -- building a positive relationship foundation.
  - **Phase 2:** Parents learn to give clear instructions and use time-out for noncompliance.
  - The child's brain gets a clear, consistent structure: positive behavior gets attention, and noncompliance has a predictable consequence.
- **Caregiver involvement:** Very high -- therapist observes and codes parent skills, and parents must demonstrate mastery before moving on.
- **Best for:** Young children with early-stage oppositional behavior. Long-term follow-up studies (up to 14 years) show lasting effects.
- **FASD note:** The mastery-based progression is a strength. However, the program was designed for "noncompliance" as a behavioral choice -- for children with FASD, apparent noncompliance is often a "can't" (brain-based) not a "won't." Caregivers and clinicians need this reframe.

#### 7. Other Notable Programs

**Families Moving Forward (FMF):**
- Specifically designed for children with FASD
- Focuses on changing the environment and caregiver expectations rather than changing the child
- Teaches caregivers to reframe behavior as reflective of brain-based differences rather than intentional noncompliance
- Includes school consultation and community connections
- This is the most FASD-specific parent training program available

**Parents and Children Together (PACT):**
- Also designed for FASD populations
- Uses the "How is your engine running?" metaphor to teach children and caregivers about arousal/regulation states
- Addresses behavior regulation, executive functioning, and parent effectiveness

**Defiant Children / Defiant Teens (Russell Barkley):**
- Structured parent training manuals widely used in clinical practice
- Strong focus on ADHD-related defiance and executive function challenges

**1-2-3 Magic (Thomas Phelan):**
- Simplified parent training approach using counting for misbehavior
- More of a psychoeducational program than a clinical treatment
- Very accessible but less depth than clinical programs

#### Collaborative and Proactive Solutions (CPS)
**Also known as:** Collaborative Problem Solving (former name); "The Explosive Child" approach
**Developer:** Ross Greene, PhD

**What it is:** A non-punitive approach based on the premise that "kids do well if they can" (not "kids do well if they want to"). Challenging behavior is understood as the result of lagging skills (flexibility, frustration tolerance, problem-solving) rather than lack of motivation.

**What it asks the brain to do:** CPS does NOT try to motivate better behavior through rewards and consequences. Instead, it:
- Identifies the specific, unsolved problems causing challenging behavior
- Teaches the child's brain to participate in solving those problems collaboratively with an adult
- Builds the child's executive function, flexibility, and problem-solving skills through real practice (not worksheets)
- Uses "Plan B" conversations: empathy step (understanding the child's concern), define-the-adult-concern step, invitation to brainstorm solutions

**Caregiver involvement:** Very high. Caregivers are taught the CPS framework and implement it at home. The caregiver shifts from trying to impose solutions (Plan A) or giving in (Plan C) to collaborating with the child (Plan B).

**How to know if it's a good fit:**
- The child has meltdowns, explosions, or extreme rigidity
- Traditional reward/consequence approaches haven't worked or have made things worse
- The child seems to struggle with flexibility and frustration tolerance, not just "won't listen"
- The family is ready to shift from a compliance-focused to a problem-solving-focused approach

**FASD relevance:** HIGH. CPS's core assumption -- that challenging behavior reflects lagging skills, not lack of motivation -- aligns perfectly with a brain-based understanding of FASD. For children whose brains genuinely cannot produce the flexibility or problem-solving demanded of them, CPS meets them where they are. The emphasis on solving specific, concrete problems (rather than abstract behavior goals) is also a good fit.

**Evidence base:** Recognized as evidence-based by the California Evidence-Based Clearinghouse for Child Welfare. Research in families, schools, group homes, inpatient units, and juvenile detention facilities.

#### Theraplay
**Developer:** Ann Jernberg; now maintained by the Theraplay Institute

**What it is:** A structured, relationship-focused play therapy modality in which the therapist (and later the caregiver) leads playful, nurturing, attuned interactions that replicate the healthy parent-infant relationship. Unlike child-centered play therapy, Theraplay is adult-directed.

**What it asks the brain to do:**
- Activates the social engagement system (the branch of the nervous system that supports connection, not fight/flight)
- Provides the sensorimotor, co-regulation experiences that build the brain's capacity for self-regulation
- Rebuilds attachment patterns through repeated experiences of attunement, playfulness, and nurture
- Works through the body and relationship, not through words or insight

**Caregiver involvement:** Very high. Caregivers observe sessions initially, then are coached to take over the Theraplay activities. The goal is to transform the caregiver-child interactions at home.

**How to know if it's a good fit:**
- The child has attachment disruptions, difficulty trusting adults, or a history of relational trauma
- The child is withdrawn, controlling, or has difficulty accepting nurture
- The child is young or has developmental delays that make talk-based therapy difficult
- You want to strengthen the caregiver-child bond specifically

**FASD relevance:** MODERATE-HIGH. Theraplay's sensorimotor, relationship-based approach works well for children whose brains are wired for "bottom-up" (body/relationship) rather than "top-down" (thinking/reasoning) interventions. Because it doesn't require verbal processing or insight, it's accessible to children with cognitive differences. The adult-directed structure also provides the predictability that FASD brains often need.

**Evidence base:** Recognized by the California Evidence-Based Clearinghouse. Studies show improvements in attachment, self-esteem, behavior, and social engagement.

#### Dyadic Developmental Psychotherapy (DDP)
**Also known as:** DDP; sometimes called "attachment-focused family therapy"
**Developer:** Dan Hughes, PhD

**What it is:** A therapy for families (especially foster/adoptive) where children have experienced developmental trauma. Built around the attitude of PACE: Playfulness, Acceptance, Curiosity, and Empathy. Uses "affective-reflective dialogue" -- conversation that includes both feeling and thinking.

**What it asks the brain to do:**
- Creates felt safety in the therapy room so the child's brain can come out of defensive mode
- Uses PACE to activate the social engagement system (safety, connection) instead of the defensive system (fight/flight/freeze)
- Helps the child's brain create new narratives about themselves and their experiences
- Builds intersubjective experience -- the feeling of being truly known and understood by another person, which organizes the developing brain

**Caregiver involvement:** Very high. The caregiver is in every session. DDP works to heal the child through the caregiver-child relationship, with the therapist supporting both.

**How to know if it's a good fit:**
- The child has a history of abuse, neglect, or multiple placement disruptions
- The child has difficulty trusting caregivers or accepting comfort
- The child is adopted, fostered, or in kinship care
- Attachment and relationship are the primary concerns (not just behavior)

**FASD relevance:** MODERATE-HIGH. DDP's relational approach is well-suited for the many children with FASD who have co-occurring attachment disruptions (many have histories of foster care, adoption, and/or prenatal substance exposure). The emphasis on the caregiver's inner state (not just techniques) helps caregivers of children with FASD manage their own frustration and grief. However, the verbal/narrative elements may need adaptation for children with significant cognitive impairments.

**Evidence base:** Recognized as evidence-based. Research supports improvements in attachment, behavioral problems, and caregiver-child relationships, particularly in foster/adoptive families.

#### Trust-Based Relational Intervention (TBRI)
**Developer:** Karyn Purvis, PhD and David Cross, PhD (TCU Institute of Child Development)

**What it is:** A trauma-informed, attachment-based intervention built on three principles: Connecting, Empowering, and Correcting. Designed originally for children from "hard places" (foster care, adoption, institutional care, prenatal substance exposure).

**What it asks the brain to do:**
- **Connecting principles:** Activate the social engagement system; build felt safety through attunement, eye contact, healthy touch, and playful engagement
- **Empowering principles:** Address the physiological needs that affect brain function -- blood sugar regulation, sensory needs, hydration, sleep. (TBRI is unique in explicitly addressing the body's physiological state as a driver of behavior.)
- **Correcting principles:** Use "connected correction" -- maintaining the relationship while setting boundaries. Teaches the brain that it can be redirected without being rejected.

**Caregiver involvement:** Extremely high. TBRI is fundamentally a caregiver-training approach. Caregivers learn all three principle sets and implement them in daily life. TBRI also trains professionals, teachers, and entire organizations.

**How to know if it's a good fit:**
- The child comes from a "hard place" (trauma, foster care, adoption, institutional care, prenatal substance exposure)
- The child has attachment difficulties, sensory needs, or regulation difficulties
- The caregiver wants a comprehensive framework that addresses body, relationship, AND behavior
- The family is adopting or fostering and wants a trauma-informed parenting framework from the start

**FASD relevance:** HIGH. TBRI was designed with prenatal substance exposure explicitly in mind. The Empowering principles directly address the blood sugar dysregulation and sensory processing issues common in FASD. TBRI's research notes that "children and youth with histories of prenatal exposure to substances often have significant changes to insulin receptor sites, making them subject to dramatic shifts in behavior when their blood sugar begins to drop." This physiological awareness is critical for FASD families and often missing from other modalities.

**Evidence base:** Growing research base. Studies show improvements in attachment security and mental health after one year. TCU Institute continues to produce research. A 2025 feasibility study of TBRI home visiting for early childhood showed promising results.

#### Child-Parent Psychotherapy (CPP)
**Developer:** Alicia Lieberman, PhD (UCSF)

**What it is:** An evidence-based treatment for children ages 0-5 who have experienced trauma or have attachment/behavioral problems. The caregiver-child relationship is the vehicle for healing.

**What it asks the brain to do:**
- Repairs the attachment relationship, which is the foundation for the young child's brain development
- Helps the child's brain process traumatic experiences within the safety of the caregiver relationship
- Helps the caregiver understand how their own history may affect their responses to the child
- Uses unstructured play and interaction to let the young child's brain express and integrate experiences it can't yet put into words

**Caregiver involvement:** Very high. The caregiver is in every session. The therapy works through the relationship.

**How to know if it's a good fit:**
- The child is under age 5
- There is a history of trauma, domestic violence, or disrupted attachment
- Behavioral problems are present alongside relationship difficulties
- The caregiver-child bond needs support

**FASD relevance:** MODERATE. CPP's relational, developmentally sensitive approach is well-suited for young children with FASD, especially those in foster/adoptive placements. The non-verbal, play-based format accommodates cognitive differences. However, it is limited to ages 0-5.

**Evidence base:** Strong. Multiple randomized controlled trials show improvements in attachment security, behavior problems, and PTSD symptoms. Recognized by multiple clearinghouses as evidence-based.

#### Attachment and Biobehavioral Catch-up (ABC)
**Developer:** Mary Dozier, PhD (University of Delaware)

**What it is:** A brief (10-session), home-based intervention for infants and toddlers who have experienced early adversity (primarily foster care and child welfare-involved families). A coach helps the caregiver learn to provide nurturing care even when the child doesn't seem to seek it.

**What it asks the brain to do:**
- Helps regulate the child's stress response system (specifically the cortisol/HPA axis, which is often dysregulated in children from adversity)
- Builds the caregiver's ability to read and respond to the child's attachment signals, even when those signals are confusing or muted
- Creates the predictable, responsive caregiving environment that the young brain needs to develop healthy self-regulation

**Caregiver involvement:** Extremely high -- it's a caregiver coaching model.

**How to know if it's a good fit:**
- The child is an infant or toddler (under age 2 primarily)
- The child is in foster care, kinship care, or has experienced early adversity
- The caregiver wants support in building a secure attachment from the start

**FASD relevance:** MODERATE. Addresses the stress-response dysregulation common in prenatal alcohol exposure. The brief format and home-based delivery remove barriers. However, it's limited to very young children.

**Evidence base:** Very strong. Level 1 rating from CEBC. Positive effects on attachment, cortisol patterns, self-regulation, language, and externalizing/internalizing behaviors.

#### Somatic Experiencing (SE)
**Developer:** Peter Levine, PhD

**What it is:** A body-based (somatic) approach to healing trauma. Based on the principle that trauma gets "stuck" in the nervous system and body, not just in thoughts and memories.

**What it asks the brain to do:**
- Works "bottom-up" -- starts with the body and nervous system rather than thoughts and stories
- Helps the child's brain complete the defensive responses (fight, flight, freeze) that got interrupted during trauma
- Teaches the child to notice and track body sensations (interoception), building the brain's capacity to regulate its own arousal
- Gently helps the nervous system discharge stored survival energy without overwhelming the child
- Specifically avoids direct, intense re-experiencing of trauma (contrasts with exposure-based approaches)

**Caregiver involvement:** Varies. Caregivers may be included in sessions or coached on how to support regulation at home. Not as consistently caregiver-involved as some other modalities.

**How to know if it's a good fit:**
- The child has trauma-related symptoms that seem "stuck in the body" (startling, freezing, physical tension, unexplained pain)
- Talk therapy or cognitive approaches haven't worked because the child can't access or describe their experience verbally
- The child has meltdowns or shutdowns that seem disproportionate to the trigger
- There is a trauma history but the child can't narrate it (common in early/preverbal trauma)

**FASD relevance:** MODERATE. The "bottom-up," body-based approach bypasses the cognitive demands that are challenging for FASD brains. For children with FASD who carry both prenatal and postnatal trauma, SE offers a way to address the nervous system directly. However, SE requires some interoceptive awareness (ability to notice internal sensations), which may be variable in children with FASD.

**Evidence base:** Growing but still limited for children specifically. Peter Levine received the Reis Davis Chair in Child Psychiatry for his work. More research is needed on SE specifically with pediatric populations.

#### Sensory Integration / Occupational Therapy (OT)
**Note:** This is not a "therapy modality" in the same way as the others -- it's a related discipline that often works alongside mental health treatment.

**What it is:** Occupational therapists trained in sensory integration work with children whose brains process sensory information differently (over-responsive, under-responsive, or sensory-seeking). This directly affects behavior.

**What it asks the brain to do:**
- Helps the brain organize and modulate sensory input so the child is not constantly in overload or underload
- Creates a "sensory diet" -- a personalized plan of sensory activities that keeps the nervous system in an optimal zone
- Builds the brain's capacity to filter, prioritize, and integrate sensory information

**Caregiver involvement:** High. Caregivers learn to implement the sensory diet at home and make environmental modifications.

**Why it matters for "big behaviors":**
- Many meltdowns and aggressive episodes are actually sensory overload responses -- the child's brain is in neurological "panic"
- Addressing sensory processing can dramatically reduce behavioral episodes that look like defiance but are actually a brain overwhelmed by input
- OT/sensory work is often a critical complement to any therapy modality

**FASD relevance:** VERY HIGH. Sensory processing differences are extremely common in FASD. Children with FASD may be over-reactive to noise, touch, visual clutter, or other input, leading to meltdowns that look behavioral but are actually sensory. An OT evaluation should be considered standard for any child with FASD who has "big behaviors."

#### Functional Family Therapy (FFT)
**Developer:** James Alexander, PhD

**What it is:** A short-term family therapy for adolescents (ages 11-18) with serious behavioral problems, including delinquency, substance use, and conduct disorders.

**What it asks the brain to do:**
- Works at the family system level -- changes family interaction patterns that maintain problem behavior
- Moves through phases: engagement/motivation, behavior change, and generalization
- Helps the adolescent brain develop new relational patterns within the family context

**Caregiver involvement:** Very high -- the entire family participates. FFT treats the family as a system.

**How to know if it's a good fit:**
- The youth is an adolescent (11-18)
- Behavioral problems are serious (delinquency, violence, substance use)
- The family is willing to participate together
- Other individual approaches haven't changed the family dynamics

**FASD relevance:** MODERATE. The family-systems approach is relevant, but FFT was designed for typically-developing adolescents. Adaptations would be needed for teens with FASD, particularly around expectations for insight and abstract reasoning.

**Evidence base:** Very strong. Over 50 studies. 35% reduction in felony recidivism. Recognized across multiple clearinghouses.

## PART 3: VERIFICATION OF EXISTING MODALITIES

### CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy)

**What it asks the brain to do:**
- Asks the brain to **notice its own thoughts** (metacognition), **evaluate whether those thoughts are accurate**, and **replace inaccurate thoughts with more helpful ones**
- Asks the brain to **connect thoughts to feelings to behavior** -- understanding the triangle
- Uses the prefrontal cortex (thinking/reasoning brain) to regulate the emotional brain
- This is a "top-down" approach -- it starts with thinking and works down to feelings and behavior

**Caregiver involvement:** Varies. Traditional CBT is often individual (child/teen and therapist). Some programs include parent sessions. Not inherently a family-based approach.

**Relevance to brain-based differences -- SIGNIFICANT LIMITATIONS:**
- CBT assumes the brain CAN engage in accurate self-monitoring, abstract reasoning, and generalization of skills to new situations
- For children with FASD, these are often the exact skills their brain struggles with most
- CBT relies heavily on verbal/language processing, working memory, and executive function -- all areas commonly affected in FASD
- Children may appear to "learn" CBT skills in session but be unable to apply them in real life (the generalization problem)
- CBT can inadvertently set children up for failure and frustration if the brain-based barrier isn't recognized
- **Bottom line for FASD:** CBT may work as one component (especially in modified/simplified forms) but should NOT be the sole approach for a child with brain-based differences. If a child isn't responding to CBT, the first question should be "Can their brain do what this therapy is asking?" not "Are they not trying hard enough?"

---

### DBT (Dialectical Behavior Therapy)

**What it asks the brain to do:**
- Teaches **four skill sets**: Mindfulness, Distress Tolerance, Emotion Regulation, and Interpersonal Effectiveness (plus "Walking the Middle Path" in the adolescent version, which helps families navigate dialectics/both-and thinking)
- Asks the brain to **notice emotions without acting on them** (distress tolerance)
- Asks the brain to **be present-focused** (mindfulness) -- this engages the prefrontal cortex in a different way than CBT; less about "correcting" thoughts and more about "observing" them
- Teaches **concrete, practical skills** for managing crises (e.g., TIPP skills for physiological regulation: Temperature, Intense exercise, Paced breathing, Progressive relaxation)
- Combines "top-down" (mindfulness, reasoning) with some "bottom-up" (body-based distress tolerance skills)

**Caregiver involvement:** The adolescent version (DBT-A) includes caregivers in the skills group. Caregivers learn the same skills as the teen. This is a significant advantage.

**Relevance to brain-based differences:**
- DBT has more concrete, behavioral skill components than standard CBT, which may be more accessible for brain-based differences
- The distress tolerance skills (ice, paced breathing, intense exercise) work on the body/nervous system level -- this bypasses some cognitive demands
- HOWEVER, full DBT is complex and demanding: weekly individual therapy + weekly skills group + between-session coaching calls + homework. This is a heavy load for any family, especially one managing FASD
- The skills training component can be adapted and simplified; some clinicians use "DBT-informed" skills teaching without the full protocol
- Mindfulness requires sustained attention and metacognition, which may be difficult for some children with FASD
- **Bottom line for FASD:** DBT skills (especially the concrete, body-based ones like distress tolerance) are valuable tools. A modified, simpler approach using selected DBT skills may work better than the full protocol. One FASD-focused program specifically recommends incorporating DBT skills into programming for adolescents with FASD.

---

### Play Therapy

**Types and what they ask the brain to do:**

**Child-Centered Play Therapy (CCPT):**
- The child leads; the therapist follows, reflects emotions, and provides unconditional positive regard
- Asks the brain to express and process emotions through play (the child's natural language) rather than words
- Works through the experiential/emotional brain -- does NOT require verbal processing, abstract thinking, or insight
- The therapist's role is to create safety and reflect, not to direct or interpret
- Children under 10 typically lack the cognitive development for abstract talk therapy; play IS how their brains process experience

**Directive Play Therapy:**
- The therapist introduces specific activities, games, or themes to address identified issues
- May use sand tray, art, puppets, or structured games to work on specific skills (emotion identification, problem-solving, etc.)
- Asks the brain to engage with specific content through accessible, concrete means

**Filial Therapy:**
- The therapist trains the CAREGIVER to conduct child-centered play sessions with their own child
- The caregiver learns to follow the child's lead, reflect emotions, and set limits
- Asks the caregiver's brain to shift from directing/managing to following/attuning
- Strengthens the caregiver-child attachment through play
- Caregiver involvement: VERY HIGH -- the caregiver becomes the therapeutic agent

**Caregiver involvement across types:** Varies dramatically. CCPT traditionally has low caregiver involvement (the therapy happens between child and therapist). Filial Therapy has very high caregiver involvement. Directive approaches vary.

**FASD relevance:** HIGH for play-based approaches generally. Play therapy works through the experiential brain, bypasses verbal/cognitive demands, and meets children at their developmental (not chronological) level. For children with FASD, play therapy may be the most accessible individual therapy modality. Filial Therapy is especially promising because it strengthens the caregiver relationship and teaches the caregiver to attune to the child's developmental level.

### EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)

**What it asks the brain to do:**
- Asks the brain to **reprocess traumatic memories** that have gotten "stuck" in the limbic system (emotional brain) without being properly filed by the prefrontal cortex
- Uses **bilateral stimulation** (eye movements, tapping, or auditory tones) while the person holds the traumatic memory in mind
- This appears to help the brain move memories from "active threat" storage to "past event" storage
- Research shows EMDR produces changes in the hippocampus, amygdala, insula, and prefrontal cortex -- the limbic/survival regions calm down and the frontal regions become more active
- The exact mechanism is still debated (theories include orienting response, working memory taxation, REM-like processing)

**Caregiver involvement:** Varies. For children, best practice includes caregiver involvement in preparation, stabilization, and support between sessions. The actual reprocessing is typically child-and-therapist. Some child EMDR protocols include the caregiver more actively.

**Relevance to brain-based differences:**
- EMDR does not require the child to talk extensively about the trauma, which is an advantage over narrative-based approaches
- EMDR does require some ability to hold a memory in mind while simultaneously tracking bilateral stimulation -- this dual-attention task requires working memory
- For children with intellectual disabilities (which can co-occur with FASD), a systematic review found that "communication, cognition, stress regulation, and attachment difficulties may interfere with the EMDR process"
- Adaptations for neurodevelopmental differences include: simplifying language, decreasing pace, involving caregivers/support staff, using the storytelling method (for younger developmental ages), and taking more time to build the therapeutic relationship
- Research is "encouraging" -- all studies reviewed showed reduction in PTSD symptoms -- but the evidence base for EMDR with intellectual/neurodevelopmental disabilities is still emerging
- **Bottom line for FASD:** EMDR can potentially be effective, especially for children with FASD who carry trauma, but it should be delivered by a clinician who understands neurodevelopmental adaptations. The child's developmental level, working memory capacity, and ability to tolerate the process should be carefully assessed. Not a first-line standalone approach for FASD.

---

### TF-CBT (Trauma-Focused Cognitive Behavioral Therapy)

**Should this be separate from CBT? YES, ABSOLUTELY.**

TF-CBT is a distinct, manualized treatment with several critical differences from standard CBT:

**What it is:** A structured, evidence-based treatment specifically for children ages 3-18 who have experienced trauma. Developed by Judith Cohen, Anthony Mannarino, and Esther Deblinger.

**Key differences from standard CBT:**
1. **Caregiver involvement is built in:** Sessions are typically 90 minutes -- 45 minutes with the child, 45 minutes with the caregiver. Caregivers receive ALL components in parallel. This is fundamentally different from standard CBT.
2. **Trauma narrative:** The child creates a structured account of their traumatic experience(s) -- this doesn't happen in standard CBT.
3. **Gradual exposure:** The child gradually approaches trauma-related thoughts, feelings, and situations in a safe, controlled way.
4. **Conjoint sessions:** The child shares their trauma narrative with the caregiver in supported sessions -- this is a powerful relational healing component.

**What it asks the brain to do:**
- Uses the PRACTICE components: Psychoeducation, Relaxation, Affective modulation, Cognitive coping, Trauma narrative, In-vivo mastery, Conjoint sessions, Enhancing safety
- Asks the brain to gradually process and integrate traumatic material rather than avoiding it
- Includes body-based (relaxation) and cognitive components
- The caregiver component helps the child's brain feel safe enough to approach difficult material

**Caregiver involvement:** VERY HIGH -- built into the model. This is a major strength.

**FASD relevance:** MODERATE. TF-CBT has the same cognitive demands as CBT (it IS a form of CBT) plus the additional demand of creating a trauma narrative. However:
- The caregiver involvement is a significant strength
- The relaxation and body-based components are more accessible
- TF-CBT can be adapted for younger developmental levels
- Some children with FASD carry significant trauma that needs processing
- A clinician experienced with both TF-CBT and neurodevelopmental differences would need to carefully assess whether the child can engage with the cognitive and narrative components
- **Bottom line:** List TF-CBT as its own modality. Note both its strengths (caregiver involvement, structured approach, trauma-specific) and its limitations (cognitive/narrative demands) for brain-based differences.

## PART 5: KEY THEMES FOR THE WEBSITE

### For caregivers to understand:

1. **"Top-down" vs. "bottom-up" matters.** Top-down approaches (CBT, standard talk therapy) ask the thinking brain to manage emotions and behavior. Bottom-up approaches (Somatic Experiencing, Theraplay, sensory integration) start with the body and nervous system. For children with brain-based differences, bottom-up approaches are often more accessible and should come first -- you have to calm the survival brain before the thinking brain can learn.

2. **Caregiver involvement is not optional for most children with brain-based differences.** The modalities with the strongest evidence and best fit for FASD are all high-caregiver-involvement. A therapist who sees your child alone for 50 minutes a week and sends them home cannot change the other 167 hours. Look for approaches where YOU are trained and supported.

3. **"Can't" vs. "won't" changes everything.** Many standard approaches are built on the assumption that the child CAN do the expected behavior and just needs the right motivation. For brain-based differences, the behavior often reflects a genuine skill deficit or neurological barrier. CPS, TBRI, and the FASD-specific programs (FMF, PACT) are built on this understanding.

4. **One modality is rarely enough.** A child with FASD might benefit from a combination: OT for sensory processing, a parent training approach for the caregiver, a play-based or somatic approach for the child's individual work, and CPS as a problem-solving framework for daily life.

5. **The therapy has to match the brain, not the diagnosis.** A child with FASD, a child with developmental trauma, and a child with autism may all benefit from similar bottom-up, high-caregiver-involvement approaches -- because their brains need similar things even though their diagnoses differ.
